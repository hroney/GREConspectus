Theory

Intro

INTRODUCTION

By using Newton's second law and given initial conditions, we are able to obtain the equations of motion of a given system and describe the motion of the system. Newton's laws can be used only if all the forces acting on the system are known; that is, the dynamical conditions are known.
In most situations, the problem are not that simple to solve by means of dynamical and initial conditions, for example, a mass that is constrained to move on a spherical surface or a bead that slides on a wire. In these situations, not only the unknown form of the forces of constraints makes the problem difficult to solve, but using the rectangular or other common used coordinates may make it impossible to tackle the problem (even if the forces of constraints were known).
Two different methods, Lagrange's equations and Hamilton's equations, have been developed to handle such problems.
First, these techniques use generalized coordinates. That is, instead of being limited to use of rectangular or polar coordinates. Furthermore, these techniques use an energy approach, having the primary advantage of dealing with scalars, rather than vectors.


The difference between Lagrange's and Hamilton's methods. In Lagrange's formalism the generalised coordinates used are position and velocity, resulting in second-order linear differential equations. In Hamilton's formalism the generalized coordinates used are position and momentum, resulting in first-order differential equations.

Generalized coordinates

GENERALIZED COORDINATES AND CONSTRAINTS

To locate the position of a particle, we need three coordinates. These coordinates could be Cartesian coordinates $x,y,z$; cylindrical coordinates $r,θ,z$; spherical coordinates $r,θ,φ$; or any other suitable coordinates. If there are some restrictions or constraints on the motion of the particle, we need less than three coordinates. For instance, if a particle is constrained to move on a plane surface, only two coordinates are sufficient.

The minimum number of coordinates needed to describe a system of $N$ particles, is $n=3N-m$
$m$ - is number of constraints, $n$ - is the number of degrees of freedom of the system.

The name generalized coordinates is given to any set of quantities that completely describes the state or configuration of a system. These $n$ generalized coordinates are customarily written as
$q_1,q_2,q_3,...,q_n$
$\text"or " q_k \text" ,where "k=1,2,3,...,n$
...

If each coordinate can vary independently of the other, the system is said to be holonomic. In a nonholonomic system, the coordinates cannot vary independently.

In analogy with Cartesian coordinates, we may define the derivatives of $q_k$, that is $q↖{.}_1,q↖{.}_2,...,$ or $q↖{.}_k$ as generalized coordinates.

Then, considering a single particle, whose rectangular coordinates $x,y,z$ are a function of the generalized coordinates $q_1,q_2,q_3$:
$\table x=x(q_1,q_2,q_3)=x({\bo q}_k) ; y=y(q_1,q_2,q_3)=y({\bo q}_k) ; z=z(q_1,q_2,q_3)=z({\bo q}_k)$
we suppose the system changes from an initial configuration given by $(q_1,q_2,q_3)$ to a neighborhood configuration given by $(q_1+δq_1,q_2+δq_2,q_3+δq_3)$. We can express the corresponding changes in the Cartesian coordinates by following relations:
$δx={∂x}/{∂q_1}δq_1+{∂x}/{∂q_2}δq_2+{∂x}/{∂q_3}δq_3=∑↙{k=1}↖n{∂x}/{∂q_k}δq_k$
with similar expression for $δy$ and $δz$, where $n$ is equal to three and the partial derivatives ${∂x}\text"/"{∂q_k},...,$ are functions of $q$'s. The value of $n$ depends on the degrees of freedom.

Considering a more general case in which a mechanical system consists of a large number of particles having $n$ degrees of freedom, we similarly get:
$δx_i={∂x_i}/{∂q_1}δq_1+{∂x_i}/{∂q_2}δq_2+{∂x_i}/{∂q_3}δq_3=∑↙{k=1}↖n{∂x_i}/{∂q_k}δq_k$

It is essential at this point to distinguish between two types of displacement: an actual displacement $d{\bo r}_i$ and a virtual (not in actual fact or name) displacement $δ{\bo r}_i$. Suppose a mass $m_i$ is acted on by an external force ${\bo F}_i$ and causes the mass $m_i$ to move from ${\bo r}_i$ to ${\bo r}_i+δ{\bo r}_i$ in a time interval $dt$. This displacement must be consistent with both the equations of motion and the equations of constraints that describe this mass system; hence such displacements are actual displacements. On the other hand, virtual displacements are consistent with the equation of constraints but do not satisfy the equations of motion or time. For example, the bob of a pendulum of length $l$ may be moved from $(l,θ)$ to $(l,θ+δθ)$ in any arbitrary time interval as long as the bob remains on the arc of a circle of radius $l$. Thus, $δ{\bo r}_i$ and $δq_i$ are the virtual displacements. We shall make use of the principle of virtual work in the following. We shall cause a virtual displacement $δ{\bo r}_i$, resulting in virtual work $δW$. Basically, in such displacements, the relative orientations and distances between the particles remain unchanged.

Generalized forces

GENERALIZED FORCES

Consider a force ${\bo F}$ that is acting on a single particle of mass $m$ and produces a virtual displacement $δ{\bo r}_i$ of the particle. The work done $δW$ by this force is given by
$δW={\bo F}·δ{\bo r}_i=F_xδx+F_yδy+F_zδz$
where $F_x,F_y,F_z$ are the rectangular components of ${\bo F}$. Expressing the displacements $δx,δy,δz$ in terms of generalized coordinates $q_k$, we may write
$δW=∑↙{k=1}↖n\(F_x{∂x}/{∂q_k}+F_y{∂y}/{∂q_k}+F_z{∂z}/{∂q_k}\)δq_k$
$=∑↙{k=1}↖nQ_kδq_k$
where $Q_k=F_x{∂x}/{∂q_k}+F_y{∂y}/{∂q_k}+F_z{∂z}/{∂q_k}$, $Q_k$ is called the generalized force associated with the generalized coordinate $q_k$. The dimensions of $Q_kδq_k$ are that of work. If the increment $δq_k$ has the dimensions of distance, $Q_k$ will have dimensions of force; if $δq_k$ has the dimensions of angle $θ$, $Q_k$ will have dimensions of torque $τ_θ$. It may be pointed out that the quantity $δq_k$ and the quantities $δx,δy,δz$ are called virtual displacements of the system because it is not necessary that such displacements represent any actual displacements.
...

The other interesting case relates to the CONSERVATIVE GENERAL FORCES. Suppose, a conservative force field is represented by a potential function $V=V(x,y,z)$. The rectangular components of a force acting on a particle are given by
$F_x=-{∂V}/{∂x},\text" " F_y=-{∂V}/{∂y},\text" " F_z=-{∂V}/{∂z}$
thus, $Q_k=F_x{∂x}/{∂q_k}+F_y{∂y}/{∂q_k}+F_z{∂z}/{∂q_k}$
$=-\({∂V}/{∂x}{∂x}/{∂q_k}+{∂V}/{∂y}{∂y}/{∂q_k}+{∂V}/{∂z}{∂z}/{∂q_k}\)$

Finally, $Q_k=-{∂V}/{∂q_k}$
This expresses the relation between a generalized force and the potential representing a conservative system.

Lagrange's equations

LAGRANGE'S EQUATIONS OF MOTION

-Single particle

LAGRANGE'S EQUATIONS OF MOTION FOR A SINGLE PARTICLE

We are interested in describing the motion of a single particle by means of equations written in terms of generalized coordinates. This leads us to Lagrange's equations. We could start with Newton's second law, ${\bo F}=m{\bo a}$. But it is convenient to start with an expression for kinetic energy $T$ in terms of Cartesian coordinates and then write $T$ in terms of generalized coordinates.

$T=1/2m\(x↖{.}^2+y↖{.}^2+z↖{.}^2\)$

since $x=x(q_1,q_2,...,q_n)=x(q)$
similarly $y=y(q)$, $z=z(q)$

We can evaluate $x↖{·}$ in terms of $q_k$ by the following procedure:
$x↖{.}={∂x}/{∂q_1}{∂q_1}/{∂t}+{∂x}/{∂q_2}{∂q_2}/{∂t}+...+{∂x}/{∂q_n}{∂q_n}/{∂t}$
$=∑↙{k=1}↖n{∂x}/{∂q_k}{∂q_k}/{∂t}=∑↙{k=1}↖n{∂x}/{∂q_k}q↖{.}_k=x↖{.}\(q,q↖{.}\)$
...

Now we are able to describe the different components of velocity in terms of generalized coordinates $q_k$ and generalized velocities $q↖{.}_k$, that is:
$x↖{.}=x↖{.}\(q,q↖{.}\)\text" , "y↖{.}=y↖{.}\(q,q↖{.}\)\text" , "z↖{.}=z↖{.}\(q,q↖{.}\)$

Rewriting the kinetic as:

$T=1/2m\[x↖{.}^2\(q,q↖{.}\)+y↖{.}^2\(q,q↖{.}\)+z↖{.}^2\(q,q↖{.}\)\]$

taking the derivative with respect to the generalized velocity $q↖{.}$,
${∂T}/{∂q↖{.}_k}=m\(x↖{.}{∂x↖{.}}/{∂q↖{.}_k}+y↖{.}{∂y↖{.}}/{∂q↖{.}_k}+z↖{.}{∂z↖{.}}/{∂q↖{.}_k}\)$
we may write ${∂x↖{.}}/{∂q↖{.}_k}={∂x}/{∂q_k}$
$⇒\text" "{∂T}/{∂q↖{.}_k}=m\(x↖{.}{∂x}/{∂q_k}+y↖{.}{∂y}/{∂q_k}+z↖{.}{∂z}/{∂q_k}\)$

Differentiating both sides of this equation with respect to $t$:
${d}/{dt}\({∂T}/{∂q↖{.}_k}\)=m\[x↖{..}{∂x}/{∂q_k}+y↖{..}{∂y}/{∂q_k}+z↖{..}{∂z}/{∂q_k}\]$
$+m\[x↖{.}{d}/{dt}\({∂x}/{∂q_k}\)+y↖{.}{d}/{dt}\({∂y}/{∂q_k}\)+z↖{.}{d}/{dt}\({∂z}/{∂q_k}\)\]$
and using the fact that $d\text"/"dt$ and $∂\text"/"∂q_k$ are interchangeable.
${d}/{dt}\({∂x}/{∂q↖{.}_k}\)={∂}/{∂q_k}\({dx}/{dt}\)={∂x↖{.}}/{∂q}$
or, $\text" "mx↖{.}{∂x↖{.}}/{∂q_k}={∂}/{∂q_k}\(1/2mx↖{·}^2\)$
also $F_x=mx↖{..}\text" , "F_y=my↖{..}\text" , "F_z=mz↖{..}$

We obtain:
${d}/{dt}\({∂T}/{∂q↖{.}_k}\)= F_x{∂x}/{∂q_k}+F_y{∂y}/{∂q_k}+F_z{∂z}/{∂q_k}$
$+{∂}/{∂q_k}\[1/2m\(x↖{.}^2+y↖{.}^2+z↖{.}^2\)\]$
using the definition of generalized force and kinetic energy,
$Q_k=F_x{∂x}/{∂q_k}+F_y{∂y}/{∂q_k}+F_z{∂z}/{∂q_k}$
$T=1/2m\[x↖{.}^2(q,q↖{.})+y↖{.}^2(q,q↖{.})+z↖{.}^2(q,q↖{.})\)]$
gives:

${d}/{dt}\({∂T}/{∂q↖{.}_k}\)=Q_k+{∂T}/{∂q_k}$

These differential equations in generalized coordinates describe the motion of a particle and are known as Lagrange's equations of motion.
...

Lagrange's equations take a much simpler form if the motion is in a conservative force field so that
$Q_k=-{∂V}/{∂q_k}$
which on substituting yields
${d}/{dt}\({∂T}/{∂q↖{.}_k}\)={∂T}/{∂q_k}-{∂V}/{∂q_k}$

Let us define a Lagrangian function $L$ as the difference between kinetic energy and potential energy; that is:
$L=T-V\text" or "L(q,q↖{.})=T(q,q↖{.})-V(q_k)$
...

It is important to know that, if $V$ is a function of the generalized coordinates and not of the generalized velocities, then
$V=V(q)\text" and "{∂V}/{∂q↖{.}_k}=0$
[If $V$ is not independent of velocity $q↖{.}$, then $V=V(q,q↖{.})$ will lead to a tensor force. Thus, we may write
$\table {∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}={∂}/{∂q↖{.}_k}(T-V)={∂T}/{∂q↖{.}_k}; {∂L}/{∂q_k}={∂}/{∂q_k}(T-V)={∂T}/{∂q_k}-{∂V}/{∂q_k}$

Substituting these results in Lagrangian equation, we get its final form:

${d}/{dt}\({∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}\)-{∂L}/{∂q_k}=0$

which are Lagrange's equations describing the motion of a particle in a conservative force field

To solve the equations, we must know the Lagrangian function $L$ in the appropriate generalized coordinates. Since energy is a scalar quantity, the Lagrangian $L$ is a scalar function. Thus, the Lagrangian $L$ will be invariant with respect to coordinate transformations. This means that the Lagrangian gives the same description of the system under given conditions no matter wich generalized coordinates are used. Thus, this equation describes the motion of a particle moving in a conservative force field in terms of any generalized coordinates.

-System of particles

LAGRANGE'S EQUATIONS OF MOTION FOR A SYSTEM OF PARTICLES

Extension the procedure to a more general case of $N$ particles, allows us to write the kinetic energy as:
$T=∑↙{i=1}↖N\[1/2m_i\(x↖{.}^2_i+y↖{.}^2_i+z↖{.}^2_i\)\]$
Since each particle has three degrees of freedom, the total number of $x_i$ coordinates needed to represent $N$ particles will be $3N$. Hence, we may write the kinetic energy of the system as:
$T=∑↙{i=1}↖{3N}1/2m_ix↖{.}^2_i$

Making almost exact procedure as for single particle, we may write:
${d}/{dt}\({∂T}/{∂q↖{.}_k}\)=∑↙{i=1}m_ix↖{..}_i{∂x_i}/{∂q_k}+∑↙{i=1}m_ix↖{.}_i{d}/{dt}\({∂x_i}/{∂q_k}\)$
${d}/{dt}\({∂T}/{∂q↖{.}_k}\)=Q_k+{∂T}/{∂q_k}\text" , "k=1,2,...,n$

${d}/{dt}\({∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}\)-{∂L}/{∂q_k}=0$

We are now in a position to illustrate the use of Lagrange's method for obtaining and solving equations for simple systems. It is convenient to do this if we use the following procedure as a guide.

1. Select a proper set of generalized coordinates to represent the configuration of the system.
2. Express the kinetic energy $T$ of the system in terms of generalized coordinates and their time derivatives (velocities).
3. If the system is conservative, express the potential energy $V$ as a function of the generalized coordinates; otherwise, find an expression for the generalized forces $Q_k$
4. Finally, using the preceding information, write Lagrange's equations of motion.
