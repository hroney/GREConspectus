Theory

Intro

INTRODUCTION

It was shown, how to describe a system by means of Lagrange's equations. For a system with $n$ degrees of freedom, we need $n$ generalized coordinates. The Lagrangian $L$ was described in terms of generalized coordinates $q_k$ and generalized velocities $q↖{·}_k$. Furthermore, if a Lagrangian were an explicit function of time, we wrote
$L(q,q↖{.},t)=L(q_1,q_2,...,q_n;q↖{.}_1,q↖{.}_2,...,q↖{.}_n;t)$

Lagrange's formalism leads to $n$ second-order differential equations.
An alternative to Lagrange's formalism is Hamilton's formalism. Hamilton's formalism is carried out in terms of generalized coordinates and generalized momenta. This is, if the Hamiltonian $H$ is an explicit function of time, then:
$H=H(q,p,t)=H(q_1,q_2,...,q_n;p_1,p_2,...,p_n;t)$

Such formalism for a system of $n$ degrees of freedom leads to $2n$ first-order differential equations. These $2n$ first-order equations are much easier to solve than $n$ second-order differential equations, as in the case of Lagrange's formalism.

Generalized momenta

GENERALIZED MOMENTA AND CYCLIC (OR IGNORABLE) COORDINATES

Firstly, we have to define the generalized momentum. As a simple example, let us consider the motion of a single particle moving with velocity $x↖{.}$ along $X-\text"axis"$. The kinetic energy of such a particle is
$T=1/2mx↖{.}^2$
Usually one defines momentum $p$ as $p=mx↖{.}$. But in the present formalism, we define the momentum $p$ to be
$p={∂T}/{∂x↖{.}}\text" or " p={∂L}/{∂x↖{.}}$

Thus, the generalized momentum $p_k$ corresponding to generalized coordinate $q_k$ as:

$p_k={∂L}/{∂x↖{.}_k}$

The generalized momentum $p_k$ is also called the conjugate momentum $p_k$ (conjugate to coordinate $q_k$). It may be pointed out that the generalized momenta do not always convey the same physical concept as we are used to in introductory physics using rectangular coordinates.
...

For a conservative system, Lagrange's equations are
${d}/{dt}\({∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}\)-{∂L}/{∂q_k}=0$
and takes the form $p↖{.}_k={∂L}/{∂q_k}$
That is the connection between the generalized momenta and the constants of motion.

Quantities that are functions of the coordinates and velocities that remain constant in time are called constants of motion.

Suppose in the expression for the Lagrangian $L$ of a system a certain coordinate $q_λ$ does not occur explicitly. In such cases,
${∂L}/{∂q_λ}=0$
Therefore, $p↖{.}_λ={d}/{dt}\({∂L}/{∂q_λ}\)=0$
which on integration yields
$p_λ={∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_λ}=\text"constant"=C_λ$
That is, whenever the Lagrangian function does not contain a coordinate $q_λ$ explicitly, the corresponding generalized momentum $p_λ$ is a constant of motion. The coordinate $q_λ$ is said to be cyclic or ignorable. Thus, we may conclude that the generalized momentum associated with an ignorable coordinate is a constant of motion of a system.

As an example, let us consider the motion of a particle in a central force field. In polar coordinates, the Lagrangian $L$ is
$L=T-V=1/2m\(r↖{.}^2+r^2θ↖{.}^2\)-V(r)$
Since $L$ does not contain the coordinate $θ$, $θ$ is cyclic (or ignorable), and the generalized momentum corresponding to $θ$ is
$p_θ={∂L}/{∂θ↖{.}}=mr^2θ↖{.}=\text"constant"$

where $p_θ$ is the angular momentum and is a constant of motion. A note of caution: $p_θ$ is a constant of motion; it does not mean that $θ↖{.}$ is constant. For example, in an elliptic orbit resulting from an inverse-square attractive force, $θ↖{.}$ varies, but $r$ also varies in such a way as to make $p_θ$ remain constant.

Symmetry principles

HAMILTONIAN FUNCTION: CONSERVATION LAWS AND SYMMETRY PRINCIPLES

The system that does not interact with anything outside the system is called closed system

There may be or may not be any interactions between the particles of the closed system. In either case, for such a closed system there are always seven constants or integrals of motion: (1) the linear momentum, which has three components, (2) the angular momentum, which also has three components, and (3) the total energy. Let us investigate the process by which these constants are arrived at from the considerations of Lagrangian of a closed system.

-Conservation of Linear Momentum

CONSERVATION OF LINEAR MOMENTUM

Consideration a Lagrangian of a closed system in an inertial reference frame, implies that the Lagrangian of a closed system in an inertial frame unaffected or is invariant.

Because of an outstanding property of an inertial system, that space is homogeneous in an inertial system or frame; that is, a closed system is unaffected by a translation of the entire system in space.

The variation in $L$ due to the variation in the generalized coordinates must be zero. That is, $L$ is invariant:

$δL=∑↙{k}{∂L}/{∂q_k}δq_k+∑↙{k}{∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}δq↖{.}_k=0$
Since we are causing only the displacement of the system, $δq_i$ are not functions of time; hence
$δq↖{.}_k=δ\({dq}/{dt}\)={d}/{dt}\(δq\)=0$
$⇒\text" "δL=∑↙{k}{∂L}/{∂q_k}δq_k=0\text" ⇒ "{∂L}/{∂q_k}=0$

${d}/{dt}\({∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}\)=0 \text" ⇒ "{∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}=\text"constant"$

This equation states that if space is homogeneous then the linear momentum $p_k$ of a closed system is constant. Since the motion of a single particle can be described by three Cartesian coordinates (or any other generalized coordinates), there will be three constants of motion, $p_x, p_y, p_z$, which are the three components of a linear momentum vector ${\bo p}_k$. In more general terms, we may make the following statement:

If the Lagrangian of a system, closed or otherwise, is invariant with respect to a translation in a certain direction, then the linear momentum of the system in that direction is constant in time.

-Conservation of Angular Momentum

CONSERVATION OF ANGULAR MOMENTUM

Another property of an inertial system is that space is isotropic in an inertial frame; that is, a closed system is unaffected by the orientation or rotation of the entire system.

This implies that the Lagrangian of a closed system remains invariant if the system is rotated through an infinitesimal angle.

The same equation
$δL=∑↙{k}{∂L}/{∂q_k}δq_k+∑↙{k}{∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}δq↖{.}_k=0$
By definition,
$p_k={∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}\text" ⇒ "p↖{.}_k={∂L}/{∂q_k}$
$⇒\text" "δL=∑↙{k}p↖{.}_kδq_k+∑↙{k}p_kδq↖{.}_k=0$

$δ{\bo r}=[δ{\bo θ},{\bo r}]\text" ⇒ " δ{\bo r}↖{.}=[δ{\bo θ},{\bo r}↖{.}]$

$δL={\bo p}↖{.}δ{\bo r}+{\bo p}δ{\bo r}↖{.}=0$

${\bo p}↖{.}[δ{\bo θ},{\bo r}]+{\bo p}[δ{\bo θ},{\bo r}↖{.}]=0$

$δ{\bo θ}[\({\bo r},{\bo p}↖{.}\)+\({\bo r}↖{.},{\bo p}\)]=0$

$δ{\bo θ}\({d}/{dt}[{\bo r},{\bo p}]\)=0$

$[{\bo p},{\bo r}]={\bo J}$
where ${\bo J}$ is the angular momentum about the given axis. Therefore,
$δ{\bo θ}{d{\bo J}}/{dt}=0$

${d{\bo J}}/{dt}=0 \text" ⇒ "{\bo J}=[{\bo p},{\bo r}]=\text"constant"$

where ${\bo J}$ has three components. In general:

If the Lagrangian remains invariant under rotation about an axis, then the angular momentum of the system about this axis will remain constant in time.

-Conservation of Energy

CONSERVATION OF ENERGY AND THE HAMILTONIAN FUNCTION

Another property of an inertial frame says, that the time is homogeneous within an inertial reference frame.

This implies that the Lagrangian of a closed system cannot be an explicit function of time. That is, in the total differential of $L$
$dL=∑↙{k}{∂L}/{∂q_k}dq_k+∑↙{k}{∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}dq↖{.}_k+{∂L}/{∂y}dt$

We must have $\text" " {∂L}/{∂t}=0$

${dL}/{dt}=∑↙{k}{∂L}/{∂q_k}{∂q_k}/{∂t}+∑↙{k}{∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}{∂q↖{.}_k}/{∂t}=0$

or
${dL}/{dt}=∑↙{k}{∂L}/{∂q_k}q↖{.}_k+∑↙{k}{∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}∂q↖{..}_k=0$

From Lagrange's equations,
${d}/{dt}\({∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}\)={∂L}/{∂q_k}$

${dL}/{dt}=∑↙{k}q↖{.}_k{d}/{dt}\({∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}\)+∑↙{k}{∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}∂q↖{..}_k$ $=∑↙{k}{d}/{dt}\(q↖{.}_k{∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}\)=0$

That is,
${d}/{dt}\(∑↙{k}q↖{.}_k{∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}-L\)=0$

Thus the quantity in parentheses must be constant in time. This constant is denoted by $H$, called the Hamiltonian $H$, and is given by (uning the definition of generalized momentum)
$H=∑↙{k}q↖{.}_k{∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}-L=∑↙{k}q↖{.}_kp↖{.}_k-L=\text"constant"$

Hence $H$ is a constant of motion if $L$ is not an explicit function of time $t$; that is ${∂L}\text"/"{t}=0$. $H$ takes a special form if we make the following two assumptions:

(I) The potential energy $V$ is independent of the velocity coordinate $V=V(q_k)$so that
${∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}={∂(T-V)}/{∂q↖{.}_k}={∂T}/{∂q↖{.}_k}$

(II) If the equations representing the transformation of coordinates do not contain time explicitly, then the kinetic energy $T$ will not only be the quadratic function of the generalized velocity, but will also be homogeneous in all its terms. [Caution: The transformation equations between the coordinates, say from a rectangular to generalized, will contain time explicitly if $q$'s are rotating with respect to the inertial coordinate (say rectangular) or if the constraints are functions of time.]
...

Now, according to Euler's theorem for a homogeneous function $f(q_1,q_2,...,q_n)$ of order $N$ in variables $(q_1,q_2,...,q_n)$,
$∑↙{k=1}↖N q_k{∂f}/{∂q_k}=Nf$

Thus, if the kinetic energy $T$ is a homogeneous quadratic function, that is, of the order $N=2$, we get
$∑↙{k=1}↖N q↖{.}_k{∂f}/{∂q↖{.}_k}=2f$

Combining all together, we obtain

$H=2T-(T-V)=T+V=E$

$H=E\text" ⇒ "E=\text"constant"$

where $E$ is the total energy and is constant. That is, under the assumption given above, (I) $V=V(q_k)$ and (II) $T$ is a homogeneous quadratic function; the constant of motion, the Hamiltonian $H$, is equal to the total energy $E$ of the system. It is very important to keep in mind that $H$ is not always equal to $E$. The different possibilities are as follows

$H$ is constant and is equal to the total energy $E$.

$H$ is constant but is not equal to the total energy $E$.

$H$ is not constant but is equal to the total energy $E$.

$H$ is not constant and is not equal to the total energy $E$.

Hamiltonian Dynamics

HAMILTONIAN DYNAMICS: HAMILTON'S EQUATIONS OF MOTION

Hamilton's equations of motion, also called the canonical equations of motion, will be derived here.Beginning with the Lagrangian $L$ as a function of generalized coordinates and generalized velocities and may be an explicit function of time; that is
$L=L(q_1,q_2,...,q_n;q↖{.}_1,q↖{.}_2,...,q↖{.}_n;t)$

The differential of $L$ is
$dL=∑↙{k=1}↖n\({∂L}/{∂q_k}dq_k+{∂L}/{∂q↖{.}_k}dq↖{.}_k\)+{∂L}/{∂t}dt$

$dL=∑↙{k=1}↖n\(p↖{.}_kdq_k+p_kdq↖{.}_k\)+{∂L}/{∂t}dt$

Add $q↖{.}_kp_k$ to the both sides of the equation and, after rearranging, we get
$d\(∑↙{k=1}↖np_kq↖{.}_k-L\)=∑↙{k=1}↖n\(q↖{.}_kdp_k-p↖{.}_kdq_k\)-{∂L}/{∂t}dt$

As before, we define the Hamiltonian function $H$ to be
$H=∑↙{k=1}↖np_kq↖{.}_k-L(q_1,q_2,...,q_n;q↖{.}_1,q↖{.}_2,...,q↖{.}_n;t)$

and previous equation takes form
$dH=∑↙{k=1}↖n\(q↖{.}_kdp_k-p↖{.}_kdq_k\)-{∂L}/{∂t}dt$

$L$ is an explicit function of $(q_1,q_2,...,q_n;q↖{.}_1,q↖{.}_2,...,q↖{.}_n;t)$. So, it is possible to express $H$ as an explicit function $(q_1,q_2,...,q_n;p_1,p_2,...,p_n;t)$. This can be done by using the relation defining generalized momenta, that is, ${∂L}\text"/"{∂q↖{.}_k}=p_k$; hence we can express $q↖{.}_k$ in terms of $p_k$. WHen this is possible, we can write
$H=H(q_1,q_2,...,q_n;p_1,p_2,...,p_n;t)$

We may write the differential of $H$ as
$dH=∑↙{k=1}↖n\({∂H}/{∂q_k}q_k+{∂H}/{∂p_k}p_k\)+{∂H}/{∂t}dt$

Comparison of the obtained equations, gives us

$q↖{.}_k={∂H}/{∂p_k}$
$p↖{.}_k=-{∂H}/{∂q_k}$

These equations are called Hamilton's equations of motion; and because of their symmetrical nature, they are also called canonical equations of motion. This procedure of describing motion by these equations is called Hanilton's dynamics
